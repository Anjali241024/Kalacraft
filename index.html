Beginner's Guide to Watercolor Painting: 10 Essential Techniques

Watercolor painting is one of the most accessible and rewarding art forms for beginners. Unlike oil or acrylic, watercolors require minimal supplies and dry quickly — making them perfect for crafters who want to explore painting as a new hobby.

What You'll Need

Before diving into techniques, you'll need a few key supplies. The quality of your paper matters enormously — always use cold-pressed watercolor paper (300gsm or heavier). For brushes, a round #8 and a flat wash brush will cover most situations.

  • Watercolor paints (student or professional grade)
  • Cold-pressed watercolor paper (140lb / 300gsm)
  • Round brushes: sizes 4, 8, and 12
  • Two water jars, a mixing palette, and paper towels
💡 Pro Tip: Invest in quality paper first — even cheap paints look better on good paper than premium paints on copy paper.

1. Wet-on-Wet Technique

This is the most magical watercolor technique. Wet your paper first, then drop color onto it and watch it bloom. It's perfect for skies, backgrounds, and creating dreamy, soft effects. You can also explore more landscape painting tutorials on CraftNest.

2. Wet-on-Dry Technique

Apply wet paint onto dry paper for crisp, controlled edges. This is ideal for details, lettering, and botanical illustration. For in-depth brush control guides, check out resources from Artists Network and Craftsy.

Blending Colors

Work while the paint is still wet for smooth transitions. For color theory basics, see our companion article on understanding pigment mixing for crafters.

The best part about watercolor is that "mistakes" often become your best happy accidents. Embrace the flow of the medium and don't overwork your washes.
